Project Overview

As a junior analyst at Cyclistic, I was asked to tackle a challenge that’s central to our growth: how do we turn weekend explorers into daily commuters? By analyzing over 5.7 million trips, I looked for the tipping point, the specific behaviors that distinguish our casual riders from our loyal annual members. My goal was to hand the marketing team a roadmap for high-impact conversion strategies.

Tools & Technology

SQL (BigQuery): Processed and cleaned over 5.7 million rows of historical trip data, including data merging (UNION) and handling missing April datasets.
Tableau: Designed an interactive executive dashboard featuring ride heatmaps, hourly usage trends, and seasonal analysis.
Spreadsheets: Performed initial data validation and documented cleaning steps for stakeholder transparency.

The Process

To ensure the data was clean, consistent, and ready for analysis, I followed these steps:

  • Data Consolidation & Auditing: I streamlined the ingestion process by batch-loading 12 monthly CSVs from Google Cloud Storage into BigQuery. Upon auditing the record counts, I identified that the April 2025 dataset failed to load correctly.

    • 🛠️ Technical Note: Resolving the "April Data Gap"

    While auditing the initial ingestion of 5.7M rows, I noticed the total volume looked lower than expected. To hunt down the leak, I ran a COUNT(*) grouped by month, and there it was, the April 2025 dataset (202504-divvy-tripdata) had completely failed to append during the automated load.

    The Solution: Instead of re-running the entire automation, I performed a manual schema-matched upload specifically for the April records. To be safe, I followed this up with a SQL validation script to cross-reference the counts and ensure I hadn't introduced any duplicates. This extra step was vital; without that April data, my seasonal trend analysis would have shown a "fake" dip in spring ridership, leading to totally inaccurate business advice.

  • Data Quality Audit: After encountering significant performance lag in Excel due to the 5.5M+ row count, I migrated the audit process to BigQuery. I wrote a comprehensive validation script to identify missing values (NULLs) and anomalies across all key fields.
    •         
        -- checking for NULLs and short trips
        SELECT 
        COUNT(*) AS total_rows,
        COUNTIF(ride_id IS NULL) AS missing_ids,
        COUNTIF(started_at IS NULL OR ended_at IS NULL) AS missing_timestamps,
        COUNTIF(start_station_name IS NULL) AS null_start_stations,
        COUNTIF(end_station_name IS NULL) AS null_end_stations,
            
        -- Identifying False Starts (trips under 60 seconds)
        COUNTIF(TIMESTAMP_DIFF(ended_at, started_at, SECOND) <= 60) AS short_trips_under_60s
        FROM `case-studies-478607.case_study_ggle.all_trips`;

    Figure 2:Results identifying NULLs.

    💡 Analytic Justification: While the audit identified over 1.2M records with missing end_station_name, I made the strategic decision to preserve this data. Because these records still contained accurate timestamps and GPS coordinates, they remain vital for calculating total trip volumes and average durations. Deleting 20% of the dataset would have resulted in a significant underestimation of total system usage.

  • Advanced Transformation & Imputation: To preserve data volume, I chose not to delete rows with missing station names. Instead, I used IFNULL to categorize them, while simultaneously calculating trip durations and extracting calendar data.
    •         
        -- Building the final cleaned table: all_trips_v2
        CREATE TABLE `case-studies-478607.case_study_ggle.all_trips_v2` AS
        SELECT 
            ride_id, rideable_type, member_casual,
            
        -- Missing Station Names 
        IFNULL(start_station_name, 'On-Street / Virtual Dock') AS start_station_name,
        IFNULL(end_station_name, 'On-Street / Virtual Dock') AS end_station_name,
        start_lat, start_lng, end_lat, end_lng, started_at, ended_at,
                        
        -- Calculate Ride Length in Minutes
        ROUND(TIMESTAMP_DIFF(ended_at, started_at, SECOND) / 60, 2) AS ride_length_minutes,
                        
        -- Day of the Week
        FORMAT_TIMESTAMP('%A', started_at) AS day_of_week,
                    
        -- Create a Trip Type Category
        CASE 
            WHEN TIMESTAMP_DIFF(ended_at, started_at, SECOND) <= 60 THEN 'False Start'
            ELSE 'Real Ride'
        END AS trip_type
        FROM `case-studies-478607.case_study_ggle.all_trips`
        WHERE TIMESTAMP_DIFF(ended_at, started_at, SECOND) >= 0;
  • Defining "False Starts": I implemented a 60-second threshold to identify non-journey data.

    Logic: Trips under 1 minute often represent maintenance checks or users encountering docking issues. I chose to label these as "False Starts" rather than deleting them immediately, allowing for a cleaner analysis of "Real Rides" while maintaining a full audit trail.

  • Data Aggregation & Optimization: To stay within Tableau's 1GB limit and ensure dashboard responsiveness, I aggregated the 5.5M+ rows into a summary table. I filtered for "Real Rides," extracted temporal features (Month, Season, Hour), and pre-calculated metrics.
    •         
        -- Build the final table for Tableau
            SELECT 
            member_casual, 
            rideable_type, 
            day_of_week, 
            FORMAT_TIMESTAMP('%B', started_at) AS month_name,
            
        -- Seasons
        CASE 
            WHEN FORMAT_TIMESTAMP('%B', started_at) IN ('December', 'January', 'February') 
                THEN 'Winter'
            WHEN FORMAT_TIMESTAMP('%B', started_at) IN ('March', 'April', 'May') 
                THEN 'Spring'
            WHEN FORMAT_TIMESTAMP('%B', started_at) IN ('June', 'July', 'August') 
                THEN 'Summer'
            ELSE 'Fall' 
        END AS season,
            
        -- Peak Hours
        EXTRACT(HOUR FROM started_at) AS start_hour,
        start_station_name, start_lat, start_lng,
            
        -- Aggregates     
        COUNT(*) AS total_trips,
        ROUND(AVG(ride_length_minutes), 2) AS avg_duration_mins
        FROM `case-studies-478607.case_study_ggle.all_trips_v2`
        WHERE trip_type = 'Real Ride'
        GROUP BY 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9;
  • Storage Optimization & Error Tracking: To stay under Tableau’s 1GB limit, I separated "False Starts" into a distinct summary table. This allowed me to analyze system friction (failed unlocks) without bloating the primary analysis dataset.
    •         
        -- False Starts
        SELECT   
            member_casual, 
            rideable_type, 
            day_of_week, 
        COUNT(*) AS total_false_starts
         FROM `case-studies-478607.case_study_ggle.all_trips_v2`
        WHERE trip_type = 'False Start'
        GROUP BY 1, 2, 3;

    Key Insights & Results

    The Commute Gap:While casual riders peak on weekends for leisure, members show heavy "Utility Usage" during weekday rush hours.

    Impact: Marketing should pivot from 'lifestyle' ads to 'commuter efficiency' messaging for high-frequency casual users.

    Geographic Filtering:Casual usage is concentrated at tourist landmarks, whereas members are spread across residential/commercial hubs.

    Impact: Conversion campaigns should be geo-fenced to residential 'commuter-belt' stations rather than tourist hotspots.

    Seasonal Conversion Window:Casual ridership is highly weather-dependent, spiking in the spring.

    Impact: The 'Membership Drive' should be launched in April (coinciding with the April data surge identified in the audit) to capture riders as they form new habits.

    Final Recommendations

    Based on the analysis, here are the top three strategic recommendations to convert casual riders into annual members:

    • Digital Marketing at "Leisure Hotspots": Focus social media and email campaigns on the top-performing stations identified in the Ride Heatmap, specifically near the lakefront and tourist attractions where casual usage is highest.
    • Weekend-to-Weekday Membership Trials: Since casual riders peak on weekends, offer a "Weekend Member" trial that provides discounted weekday rates, encouraging them to test the bikes for daily commuting—the primary behavior of annual members[cite: 2].
    • Seasonal "Spring-Start" Promotions: Launch targeted conversion campaigns in late March and April to coincide with the identified seasonal surge in ridership, offering a "First Year Member" discount for new sign-ups during the spring transition[cite: 2].
