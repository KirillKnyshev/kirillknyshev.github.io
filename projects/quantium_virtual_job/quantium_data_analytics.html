Project Overview

As part of the Quantium Data Analytics simulation, I analyzed retail transaction data for a major supermarket (Woolworths) to uncover the drivers of "Chips" category sales. By examining over 260,000 transactions, I identified the most profitable customer segments and evaluated the success of a new store layout trial. My goal was to provide data-backed recommendations for shelf optimization and targeted marketing.

Tools & Technology

SQL (BigQuery): Cleaned and joined transaction data with customer loyalty profiles to create a unified dataset.
Tableau: Designed 2 interactive dashboards to visualize market share, segment growth, and A/B test results (Trial vs. Control stores).

The Process

To ensure the analysis focused on typical consumer behavior, I followed a rigorous data preparation workflow:

  • Outlier Removal: Identified and removed commercial-scale transactions (e.g., single loyalty cards purchasing hundreds of packs) that would have skewed the average spend per customer.
  • Segment Merging: Joined the transaction data with the "Life Stage" and "Premium Status" tables to understand who was buying what.

    • 🛠️ Technical Note: Solving the "Top N" Filter Conflict"

    While building the Tableau dashboard, I encountered a common technical hurdle: clicking a brand filter would cause the "Top Products" chart to go blank.

    The Problem: The "Top 10" filter was calculated before the Brand filter, resulting in an empty set if the selected brand wasn't in the global top 10.

    The Solution: I applied a Context Filter to the Brand field. By forcing the Brand filter into the "Context" layer, I ensured Tableau filtered for the brand first, and then calculated the Top 10 products within that specific brand—creating a truly dynamic user experience.

    💡 Analytic Justification: While the audit identified over 1.2M records with missing end_station_name, I made the strategic decision to preserve this data. Because these records still contained accurate timestamps and GPS coordinates, they remain vital for calculating total trip volumes and average durations. Deleting 20% of the dataset would have resulted in a significant underestimation of total system usage.

  • Advanced Transformation & Imputation: To preserve data volume, I chose not to delete rows with missing station names. Instead, I used IFNULL to categorize them, while simultaneously calculating trip durations and extracting calendar data.
    •         
        -- Building the final cleaned table: all_trips_v2
        CREATE TABLE `case-studies-478607.case_study_ggle.all_trips_v2` AS
        SELECT 
            ride_id, rideable_type, member_casual,
            
        -- Missing Station Names 
        IFNULL(start_station_name, 'On-Street / Virtual Dock') AS start_station_name,
        IFNULL(end_station_name, 'On-Street / Virtual Dock') AS end_station_name,
        start_lat, start_lng, end_lat, end_lng, started_at, ended_at,
                        
        -- Calculate Ride Length in Minutes
        ROUND(TIMESTAMP_DIFF(ended_at, started_at, SECOND) / 60, 2) AS ride_length_minutes,
                        
        -- Day of the Week
        FORMAT_TIMESTAMP('%A', started_at) AS day_of_week,
                    
        -- Create a Trip Type Category
        CASE 
            WHEN TIMESTAMP_DIFF(ended_at, started_at, SECOND) <= 60 THEN 'False Start'
            ELSE 'Real Ride'
        END AS trip_type
        FROM `case-studies-478607.case_study_ggle.all_trips`
        WHERE TIMESTAMP_DIFF(ended_at, started_at, SECOND) >= 0;
  • Defining "False Starts": I implemented a 60-second threshold to identify non-journey data.

    Logic: Trips under 1 minute often represent maintenance checks or users encountering docking issues. I chose to label these as "False Starts" rather than deleting them immediately, allowing for a cleaner analysis of "Real Rides" while maintaining a full audit trail.

  • Data Aggregation & Optimization: To stay within Tableau's 1GB limit and ensure dashboard responsiveness, I aggregated the 5.5M+ rows into a summary table. I filtered for "Real Rides," extracted temporal features (Month, Season, Hour), and pre-calculated metrics.
    •         
        -- Build the final table for Tableau
            SELECT 
            member_casual, 
            rideable_type, 
            day_of_week, 
            FORMAT_TIMESTAMP('%B', started_at) AS month_name,
            
        -- Seasons
        CASE 
            WHEN FORMAT_TIMESTAMP('%B', started_at) IN ('December', 'January', 'February') 
                THEN 'Winter'
            WHEN FORMAT_TIMESTAMP('%B', started_at) IN ('March', 'April', 'May') 
                THEN 'Spring'
            WHEN FORMAT_TIMESTAMP('%B', started_at) IN ('June', 'July', 'August') 
                THEN 'Summer'
            ELSE 'Fall' 
        END AS season,
            
        -- Peak Hours
        EXTRACT(HOUR FROM started_at) AS start_hour,
        start_station_name, start_lat, start_lng,
            
        -- Aggregates     
        COUNT(*) AS total_trips,
        ROUND(AVG(ride_length_minutes), 2) AS avg_duration_mins
        FROM `case-studies-478607.case_study_ggle.all_trips_v2`
        WHERE trip_type = 'Real Ride'
        GROUP BY 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9;
  • Storage Optimization & Error Tracking: To stay under Tableau’s 1GB limit, I separated "False Starts" into a distinct summary table. This allowed me to analyze system friction (failed unlocks) without bloating the primary analysis dataset.

        -- False Starts
        SELECT   
            member_casual, 
            rideable_type, 
            day_of_week, 
        COUNT(*) AS total_false_starts
         FROM `case-studies-478607.case_study_ggle.all_trips_v2`
        WHERE trip_type = 'False Start'
        GROUP BY 1, 2, 3;

    Key Insights & Results

    The Powerhouse Segment: "Mainstream Young Singles/Couples" and "Mainstream Retirees" contribute the highest total sales volume.

    Impact: Inventory management should prioritize "Premium" shelf real estate for Kettle and Doritos to capture higher-margin sales from less price-sensitive shoppers.

    Brand Loyalty: Smiths remains the dominant brand, but Doritos and Kettle show higher-than-average spend per transaction among premium customers.

    Impact: The category reset proved that logical product grouping (pairing popular brands with high-traffic flow) significantly increases unplanned purchases, justifying a national rollout.

    The Trial Win: The store layout trial in Store 77 resulted in a massive +71.7% sales uplift compared to its control store during the peak month.

    Impact: The 'Membership Drive' should be launched in April (coinciding with the April data surge identified in the audit) to capture riders as they form new habits.

    Final Recommendations

    • Targeted Bundling:: Since "Mainstream Young Singles" prefer 175g packs, launch cross-promotional bundles with dips to increase basket size.
    • Shelf Optimization: Expand the shelf space for Smiths and Kettle in high-traffic "Mainstream" stores to maximize throughput.
    • National Rollout: Based on the successful trial in Store 77, recommend a phased rollout of the new category layout across all high-density urban locations.
